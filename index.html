Radiometer Side Up or Radiometer Side Down?

Quantifying radiation shield effectiveness on temperature sensors: the PHRSC experiment

A data narrative by Fox Peterson, with epic help from Don Henshaw and all the data managers and field techs at the H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest

This machine was so modern, so frightfully new, that nobody knew just what it would do! - Dr. Seuss, The Better Butter Battle Book

At the H.J. Andrews, we have collected air temperature over 50 + years on mountainous terrain:

Our six current primary "met" stations are in red and 30 + ancillary stations are in blue. Focus on the red

Some challenges with quality control on historical meteorological data:
  • historical v. current regional climates and microclimates
  • equipment used v. current equipment (shelters, gages, digitizers)
  • data quality and resolution (daily, five minute, instantaneous)
  • data storage and extraction (databases, field logs, files)

Which meteorlogical stations and methods are normal? And why?

The mean temperature for September 2nd's and 15:00 hours from 1691 other annually synchronous measurements is 23.4 C. Not the 43 from 1988, but not the 13.2 from 2004 either!

No QC flags were triggered on September 2, 1988. But 43 C is hot. But is it an anomaly due to the data quality? Or the data itself? How do we know what is normal when we have 45 different air temperature methods?

For example, 14 years later (May, 2002), on that same site, at that same height, another temperature sensor read 42.9 C and triggered QC flags. Was this due to time of year, method, or the data itself?

We're used to comparing observations across concurrent observations in space, but its difficult to juxtapose data across time. If only we had an experiment to help us with that ...

Oh wait, we do...

... we have

PHRSC*

* the PRIMET Historic Radiation Shield Comparison

The PRIMET HISTORIC RADIATION SHIELD COMPARISON SITE contains

  • Air temperature probes (CS107) and integrated temperature-relative humidity probes (HMP45C)
  • Net radiometer (measures long-wave and short-wave radiation, incoming and outgoing).
  • Propellor anemometer (winds < 1 m/s questionable)

Specifically, at PHRSC we set up replicas of the protective shields that we installed around H.J. Andrews "met" sites since the 1950's. At that time, we only collected daily data.

Now, we collect air temperature data from all our shield types concurrently at one site and at 5 minute resolution

To be honest, radiation shield is sort of a misleading name.

Direct radiation is only part of the problem; once addressed, we have to deal with temperature effects from convection and reflection

In other words, radiation shields have to allow air circulation while protecting the sensor from both light (generating heat) from all angles

Enclosing the sensor in reflective housing limits air circulation.

Couple that with high humidity and weak sub-canopy wind, and we've got prime conditions for convection to affect the very sensor we're trying to protect.

The literature sets 3 C as a quality threshold difference between an aspirated "truth" (a powered shield with forced air circulation) and "inferior" shields (which are better for deployment in remote locations)

4518 values on PHRSC over the past 4.5 years have been at least 3 C different than the aspirated . 248 of those have been at least 5 C different than the aspirated. ALL were greater than the aspirated.

The PVCHJA methods have more than 70% of the "low-quality" values at PHRSC, and they make up nearly 40% of the site history!

☀ There's also significant differences between the Gill "large" and "short" method (12-plated shield, 10-plated shield) and the Gill "AF" (filter) method.

☀ More Gill shields performed very poorly (outside the 5 C difference range) than did HJA shields, relative to the number that were at least 3 C different.

But the cotton shield was suprisingly similar to the aspirated.

The expectation was that the cotton shield would have the greatest number and magnitude of low-quality values

Otherwise known as Stephenson shields, cotton shields are basically double-louved protective "boxes" that originally deployed in Hyde Garden, London, in the 1630's!

Note that cotton shields fully enclose the temperature sensor, even underneath.

Would this protect the sensor more, or alter its data for the worse?

Of the 2849 unique 5 minute times where temperature difference is > 3 C by at least one of the other sensors...

Only 4 of those times have wind speeds greater than 0.5 m/s. The air is relatively stagnant, regardless of sensor housing.

Less than one percent of the down-welling solar values during this time were > 900 Watts per square meter; in fact,none were even in the top 15% for all values during the PHRSC's deployment

So if the wind is minimal , and the solar exposure is consistent, what's happening?

Here's where it gets interesting: nearly all of the most extreme

differences occur during the last week of February or the first week of March or during the beginning of September.

(this is split mostly towards March on all of the shields except the cotton, where it is split equally between seasons)

That these differences from the aspirated are greatest almost exactly six months apart may be a clue that the solar cycle may be involved

Radiometer up : the PHRSC site has a solar azimuth of approximately 218 degrees on this day in both seasons , and during the afternoon hours between 12:00 and 15:00 PM when many of the greatest differences from the aspirated sensor occured, the solar elevation angle is approximately 38 degrees.Maybe there is some trigonometry at work?

  • Distance from the PHRSC to edge to edge of forest at ~215 degree azimuth:~270m
  • Solar angle:~38 degrees
  • Minimum tree height required to intercept sun:84m

Recall that the imposed 3 C threshold is absolute (not normalized based on the maximum of the day). So seasonal radiation patterns in March and September that decrease the quality of air temperature readings relative to the aspirated shield are not necessarily coupled with general "hotness"

In short, when we look radiometer side "up", the solar regime at our site indicates the potential for an extended duration of incident radiation.

But a net radiometer also has a "side down", and maybe we are looking in the wrong direction...

Why does the enclosed cotton shield have so many less "bad values" than the bottom-ventilated shields?

And why does it have (less overall but) almost equal "bad values" through both parts of the year?

In short, what is the benefit to being enclosed, and why is it not seasonal?

In Oregon, March is in the middle of the rainy season, while September follows a long, dry, summer

One thing about rain -- it makes things, like the grassy surface underneath the PHRSC experiment -- reflective. And one thing about the controlled experiments in the literature which specify the 3 C threshold : they also specify that the surface beneath a non-aspirated temperature probe must be dry and dirt, else albedo will affect the probe

Thankfully, we looked down.

For non-night (after 5 pm and before 9 am) hours in the years 2011-2014, the number of 5-minute measurements of outgoing radiation that is greater than simultaneous incoming radiation is graphed by numerical month.

In the dry summer, almost no daytime out-going radation exceeds incoming; but in the winter, outgoing radiation can (and does) exceed incoming. For scale, 2011-2014 January has 4471 5-minute values where outgoing daytime radiation exceeds incoming.

In fact, in PRIMET's history, less than 10% of relative humidity values in March are less than 90%. In September, 79% of relative humidity values are less than 90%, so if you follow this thought, you might suggest...

So we might hypothesize that rain coupled with heat from radiation creates albedo effects and increases local humidity,

which could lead to an open-bottomed shield being more a problem than a protection

So the moral of this data "story": we may never know if 43 C is a normal temperature for a September at PRIMET. But we do know that our perception of "normal" is based on our equipment, and the various shields we've had installed for the past 30 + years have not necessarily told the site's story from the same perspective.
